Mr. Pig’s Picnic

  • Beginner
  • Children's Stories: Beginner

I love it when I find a longer story that’s suitable for beginning readers. This one, which is either a tale that reminds us what happens when you try to be something you’re not, or a tale that reminds us that men should never be allowed to give each other fashion advice, definitely fits the bill.

The first sentence isn’t super simple, so push through that one if you can, it gets much easier in the following paragraphs. Four points of grammar:

Néng Yǒu Xìng Qǐng néng yǒu xìng qǐng – A politely formal phrase of invitation “May have the pleasure of inviting you …” or more specifically, “May I be so lucky as to…” (have this dance?) (take you on a date?).

Kàn Shàng Qù kàn shàng qù – To look like.

Nǐ Yào Shì Zài Bù Zǒu Kāi nǐ yào shì zài bù zǒu kāi – This one might be a little confusing. Let’s break it down:

Nǐ – You
Yào Shì – If
Zài Bù – Otherwise / If not
Zǒu Kāi – Go away

The full phrase means “If you don’t go away….”

Tā Huì Lái Shōu Shí Nǐ De tā huì lái shōu shi nǐ de. In the story, Miss Pig says this to an unwelcome guest. The “Tā ” in this case is Mr. Pig. The Beginners often learn the word “Shōu Shí ”, meaning “to tidy up”. Usually, this means exactly what it sounds like, and applies to cleaning up a room or an apartment. In this case, it’s being used in kind of an action movie way, meaning “He’ll come over and deal with you!” or “He’ll come straighten you out!”. So Miss Pig is threatening the unwelcome guest, telling him that Mr. Pig will come over and beat him up.

The Chinese title is Zhū Xiān Shēng Qù Yě Cān

Yě Cān – yě cān – Picnic
Qī Dài – qī dài – To look forward to
Dǎ Dòng – dǎ dòng – To move someone(emotionally)
Yù Dào – yù dào – To run into
Jiè – jiè – Lend, borrow
Jiē Zhe – jiē zhe – Continuing on
Wēi Měng – wēi měng – Bold and powerful
Yīng Jùn – yīng jùn – Handsome
Wài jì Dòng – jī dòng – Excite, agitate
Yāo Guài – yāo guài – Monster, devil
Chǒu Bā Guài – chǒu bā guài – Ugly person

Xiànzài Tiān ，Zhēn Shì Gè Yě Cān De Hǎo Rì Zi 。 Zhū Xiān Shēng Jīng Xīn Dǎ Bàn Zhe Zì Jǐ ，Tā Qī Dài Zhe Zhū Xiǎo Jiě Néng Yǔ Tā Yī Qǐ Qù Yě Cān 。

“Ā Ā ，Zhēn Xī Wàng Tā Huì Shuō ‘Wǒ Yuàn Yì ’A 。 Ń ，Wǒ Zài Zhāi Duǒ Huā Sòng Gěi Tā ，Yī Dìng Néng Gòu Dǎ Dòng Tā ！”

Lù Shàng ，Zhū Xiān Shēng Yù Dào Le Tā De Péng Yǒu Hú Lí 。Hú Lí Tīng Shuō Le Yě Cān De Shì ，Jiù Shuō ：“Ràng Wǒ Gěi Nǐ Yī Gè Jiàn Yì ，Bǎ Wǒ Měi Lì De Wěi Bā Jiè Qù Ba 。Qiáo ，Nǐ Kàn Shàng Qù Yǒu Duō Cōng Míng A ，Zhū Xiǎo Jiě Kěn Dìng Huì Xǐ Huān De 。” Zhū Xiān Shēng Hěn Mǎn Yì 。

Jiē Zhe ，Tā Yòu Yù Dào Le Tā De Péng Yǒu Shī Zi 。Shī Zi Tīng Shuō Le Yě Cān De Shì ，Jiù Shuō ：“Ràng Wǒ Gěi Nǐ Yī Gè Jiàn Yì ，Bǎ Wǒ Měi Lì De Tóu Fā Jiè Qù Ba 。 Qiáo ，Nǐ Kàn Shàng Qù Yǒu Duō Wēi Měng A ，Zhū Xiǎo Jiě Kěn Dìng Huì Xǐ Huān De 。” Zhū Xiān Shēng Hěn Mǎn Yì 。

Hòu Lái ，Tā Yòu Yù Dào Tā De Péng Yǒu Bān Mǎ 。Bān Mǎ Tīng Shuō Le Yě Cān De Shì ，Jiù Shuō ：“Ràng Wǒ Gěi Nǐ Yī Gè Jiàn Yì ，Bǎ Wǒ Měi Lì De Tiáo Wén Jiè Qù Ba 。 Qiáo ，Nǐ Kàn Shàng Qù Yǒu Duō Yīng Jùn A ，Zhū Xiǎo Jiě Kěn Dìng Huì Xǐ Huān De 。” Zhū Xiān Shēng Hěn Mǎn Yì 。Tā Jué Dé Zì Jǐ Cóng Lái Méi Yǒu Zhè Yàng Yīng Jùn Guò 。

Zhōng Yú Lái Dào Zhū Xiǎo Jiě Jiā Le ，Zhū Xiān Shēng Wài jì Dòng De Qiāo Le Qiāo Mén 。“Néng Yǒu Xìng Qǐng Nǐ Yī Qǐ Qù Yě Cān Ma ？”Tā Wèn 。

Zhū Xiǎo Jiě Xià Le Yī Dà Tiào ：“Ō ，Bù Xíng ！Nǐ Shì Nǎ Er Lái De Yāo Guài Ya ？Nǐ Yào Shì Zài Bù Zǒu Kāi ，Wǒ Jiù Qù Jiào Zhū Xiān Shēng Le ，Tā Huì Lái Shōu Shí Nǐ De ！”

Zhū Xiān Shēng Lián Máng Wǎng Shíjiān Pǎo 。Yī Lù Shàng ，Tā Bǎ Tiáo Wén Hái Gěi Le Bān Mǎ ，Bǎ Tóu Fā Hái Gěi Le Shī Zi ，Bǎ Wěi Bā Hái Gěi Le Hú Lí 。Rán Hòu ，Tā Yòu Gǎn Shíjiān Dào Zhū Xiǎo Jiě De Jiā ，Zài Yī Cì Èn Xiǎng Le Mén Líng 。

“Néng Yǒu Xìng Qǐng Nǐ Yī Qǐ Qù Yě Cān Ma ？”Tā Yòu Wèn 。

“A Ya ，Zhū Xiān Shēng ！” Zhū Xiǎo Jiě Jiào Dào ，“Kàn Dào Nǐ Wǒ Zhēn Shì Tài Gāo Xìng La ，Wǒ Hěn Yuàn Yì Gēn Nǐ Yī Qǐ Qù Yě Cān 。Gāng Cái Lái Le Gè Chǒu Bā Guài ，Jiù Zhàn Zài Wǒ De Yuàn Zi Lǐ ，Kě Bǎ Wǒ Xià Huài La 。”

Yī Lù Shàng ，Zhū Xiǎo Jiě Bǎ Nà Gè Chǒu Bā Guài De Gù Shì Zǐ Xì De Jiǎng Gěi Le Zhū Xiān Shēng 。Tā Yīng Jùn De Péng Yǒu Zhū Xiān Shēng ，Zé Yī Zhí Mǎn Huái Tóng Qíng De Tīng Zhe 。Zhè Zhēn Shì Yī Gè Qù Yě Cān De Hǎo Rì Zi A 。

SHOW ENGLISH TRANSLATION »
Today, it’s a great day for a picnic. Mr. Pig dressed himself with care, looking forward to [the possibility of] Miss Pig and himself going on a picnic together.

“Heh, I hope she says ‘Yes’ [lit: I'm willing]. Hm, I’ll also pick some flowers to give her, that will certainly be enough to sway her!”

On the road, Mr. Pig ran into his friend Fox. When fox heard about the picnic, he said: “Let me give you a suggestion, let me lend you my beautiful tail. See, you look so clever, Miss Pig will certainly like it.” Mr. Pig was very pleased.

Continuing on, he ran into his friend Lion. Lion heard about the picnic and said: “Let me give you a suggestion, borrow my beautiful hair for when you go over there. See, you look so powerful, Miss Pig will certainly like it.” Mr. Pig was very pleased.

After a while, he ran into his friend Zebra. Zebra heard about the picnic and said: “Let me give you a suggestion, I’ll lend you my beautiful stripes. See, you look so handsome. Miss Pig will certainly like it.” Mr. Pig was very pleased. He felt that he’d never before looked so handsome.

Finally arriving at Miss Pig’s house, Mr. Pig excitedly knocked on the door. “Would you do me the honor of going on a picnic with me?” he asked.

Miss Pig was terrified: “Oh, no way! What kind of monster are you? If you don’t go away, I’ll go get Mr. Pig, he’ll straighten you out!”

Mr. Pig hurriedly ran away. On the road, he gave the stripes back to Zebra, the hair back to Lion, and the tail back to Fox. AFter that, he hurried back to Miss Pig’s house, and again pressed the doorbell.

“Would you do me the honor of having a picnic with me?” he asked again.

“Goodness, Mr. Pig!” Miss Pig cried out. “I’m so happy to see you, I’d love to go on a picnic with you. An ugly wretch just came by here, standing in my garden, and scared me so.”

On the way, Miss Pig told Mr. Pig the story of the ‘ugly wretch’ in great detail. Her handsome friend Mr. Pig, though, listened with great sympathy. It really was a great day for a picnic.

    May I suggest what one native speaker confirmed as a couple useful corrections:

    Wǒ Zài Zhāi Duǒ Huā ， translated here as “I’ll also pick some flowers to give her.”

    1. In spoken Chinese, the number 1 very often gets omitted, so that the measure word/classifier does not have the number which frequently precedes it. Here, it is one flower being picked; if it were some flowers, it would probably be “Zhāi Xiē Huā ”
    2. Zài implies that the action is being repeated, whereas translating it as “also” suggests that flowers are being picked for the first time. So a better way to translates this would be “I will pick another flower to give her

    • Reply Kendra says: May 28, 2014 at 5:54 am

      I don’t think the issue here is with the object – in both cases, the sentences have an object. What they do not have is the actual act of physically picking something up (taking it) literally and doing something with it.

      In both of your example sentences, what is being taken is figurative.

      Kě Bǎ Wǒ Xià Huài La – This is a very similar type of usage as the English phrase “The sight of that stunning scenery really grabbed me.” In this case, no one was actually grabbed. Same deal here. I’m not a grammarian, so I can’t explain it to you in those terms, but if you study the actual make-up of the word Xià Huài , you’ll see why “Bǎ ” makes sense. These two characters together – Xià Huài – mean “to really frighten”. But separately, they mean Xià – “to startle” and Huài – “to break”. You have to “take” something (Bǎ ) before you can startle it so bad you break it. Xià Huài is actually an act-result type of word. Because Xià happened (I was started), then Huài happened.

      Same thing here – Zhū Xiǎo Jiě Bǎ Nà Gè Chǒu Bā Guài De Gù Shì Zǐ Xì De Jiǎng Gěi Le Zhū Xiān Shēng – What is being “taken” is the story (Gù Shì ), so Gù Shì is the object. But then what is being done with the story? It’s being GIVEN (Jiǎng Gěi ) to Mr. Pig. You have to TAKE something (pick it up, figuratively have it) before you can GIVE it.
